Case study — Enterprise SaaS

Audit preparation:
three weeks,
then three days.

A SaaS business past $200M in ARR was still recognising revenue in spreadsheets, with IPO diligence approaching. We moved ASC 606 into the system — multi-element arrangements, variable consideration, contract modifications — and left them with an audit trail that runs from a revenue line back to the order that created it.

Sector Enterprise SaaS
Scale $200M+ ARR
Scope ASC 606 automation
Client Anonymised
Practice Quote-to-Cash

01 — The challenge

The close was a spreadsheet

The spreadsheets worked. That was the problem. They had been extended every quarter for years by people who understood ASC 606 extremely well, and they produced numbers the company believed. But the logic lived in formulas rather than in a system, the reasoning lived in the heads of two or three people, and none of it would survive contact with an IPO diligence process that asks you to prove the same answer twice.

  • Multi-element arrangements were being decomposed into performance obligations by hand, with the standalone selling price analysis maintained in a separate workbook.
  • Variable consideration and its constraint were estimated outside any system, so the estimate and the revenue it drove were only linked by a person remembering to update both.
  • Contract modifications were the worst of it. Whether a change was a separate contract, a prospective reallocation or a cumulative catch-up was decided case by case, and the same case did not always get the same decision.
  • The deferred revenue waterfall and the roll-forward were rebuilt manually each period, which meant the close could not start until one specific person was available.
  • Audit preparation took three weeks, most of it spent reconstructing why a number was what it was rather than demonstrating that it was right.
  • There was no trail from a recognised revenue line back to the order that created it — the exact thing a diligence process asks for first.

02 — What we built

04 workstreams
01

Performance obligations, in configuration

We took the five steps of ASC 606 and made each one a system decision rather than a judgement call repeated monthly. Contract identification and combination; decomposition into performance obligations; the transaction price, including variable consideration and its constraint; standalone selling price analysis with the resulting allocation and carve-outs; and recognition against each obligation as it is satisfied. The SSP methodology was written down as a memo the auditor could read, and then encoded, so the logic and the documentation describe the same thing.

02

Contract modifications, decided once

This is the work nobody budgets for and everybody needs. We built the decision logic that determines whether a modification is a separate contract, a prospective reallocation or a cumulative catch-up, and made the system apply it consistently — so the same contract change presented twice produces the same accounting twice. Judgement is still required at the edges. It is now required once, recorded, and reused, rather than re-litigated every quarter under close pressure.

03

The waterfall and the roll-forward

Recognition schedules, the deferred revenue waterfall, the split between contract asset and contract liability, and the roll-forward the auditor asks for on the first morning — all generated from the underlying contract records rather than assembled from them. The month-end rev-rec close stopped depending on the availability of one person, which is where 90% of its duration was hiding.

04

An audit trail that goes all the way back

Every recognised revenue line now traces to the performance obligation it belongs to, the allocation that sized it, the contract that created it, and the order behind that. Diligence questions stopped being research projects. The team could answer them from the system in the meeting, which is the difference between three weeks of preparation and three days.

Read the first two numbers carefully, because they are narrower than they look. 100% is not a claim that revenue can never be wrong. It is the accuracy of the recognition engine against the contracts it was given, and it is corroborated by the fact that the first full audit cycle after go-live raised zero findings against revenue recognition. A bad contract still produces bad revenue; the system only guarantees that the same contract always produces the same answer.

And the 90% applies to the revenue recognition portion of the close, not to the close as a whole. The rest of the month-end — the parts we were not engaged on — takes exactly as long as it used to.
