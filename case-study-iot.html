Case study — IoT platform

Annual churn:
28%, then 13%.
It was a billing problem.

An IoT platform billing more than 15,000 devices on usage was losing more than a quarter of its customers every year. The product was not the reason. The invoice was. Customers could not predict what they would be charged, and a customer who cannot forecast your bill does not renew it.

Sector IoT platform
Scale 15,000+ devices
Scope Usage billing & renewals
Client Anonymised
Practice Quote-to-Cash

01 — The challenge

Churn that looked like product

The company had been reading its 28% churn as a product signal and staffing accordingly. What the data actually showed was narrower and more fixable: customers were leaving after an invoice they had not expected, and the renewal conversation was starting at a point in the term where there was nothing left to say.

  • Usage was rated and billed in arrears with no visibility during the period. The first time a customer saw their consumption was on the invoice.
  • There was no ceiling. A month of unusual device activity produced a bill nobody had budgeted for, and no mechanism existed to cap or smooth it.
  • Disputed invoices were a standing queue. Each one consumed finance time, delayed cash, and left a customer who now distrusted the meter.
  • Renewals were reactive. The conversation began at, or after, the contract end date — by which point the decision to leave had usually already been taken.
  • Nobody could see a churn signal early. Falling device activity, rising disputes and a quiet account were all visible in the data and connected to nothing.
  • Upsell at renewal was ad hoc, because the account team had no view of a customer's actual usage trajectory against what they had committed to.

02 — What we built

04 workstreams
01

The usage path, rebuilt

Everything downstream depends on the meter being trusted, so we started there: ingestion from 15,000+ devices, deduplication, aggregation, and a rating run that can be re-run and produce the same result. Then we exposed it. Customers see consumption against their commitment during the period, not after it. Most of the disputes queue disappeared not because the bills changed, but because the bills stopped being a surprise.

02

Committed use and overage protection

We introduced commercial structures the old billing configuration could not express: committed-use discounts that reward a customer for forecasting honestly, and overage protection that puts a known ceiling on a bad month. This is a pricing change as much as a systems change, and it was the one the client hesitated over longest — it trades a little revenue from spike months for a customer who can plan. The churn number is what that trade bought.

03

Renewals that start 90 days out

Renewal workflows now trigger 90 days before contract end, not at it. The account team gets the renewal quote, the customer's usage trajectory against their commitment, and the open items on the account, in one place, while there is still time to act on any of it. A renewal that begins on the end date is not a renewal conversation. It is an exit interview.

04

Churn signals, connected to something

The signals already existed — declining device activity, repeated disputes, consumption drifting below commitment. We wired them into the renewal workflow so that an account trending the wrong way surfaces months before the date, to a person whose job it is to do something about it. The same view drives upsell in the other direction: a customer consistently over their commitment is a conversation, not an overage charge.

Churn at 13% is not churn solved. It is churn halved, and the remaining thirteen percent includes customers who left for reasons no billing system was ever going to address — the wrong product, a changed strategy, a business that stopped existing. We claim the fifteen points. We do not claim the rest.

The $3.2M is retained ARR, not new ARR: revenue the previous churn rate would have taken out, measured over the same twelve months. Committed-use discounts and overage caps cost the client something in spike-month revenue to get it. That trade was made deliberately, and it was the right one, but it was a trade.
