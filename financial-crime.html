01 — The through-line
One practice is about money leaking out of a business. The other is about money leaking into it. Both are failures of the same joint.

Quote-to-Cash breaks where the CRM hands a deal to the billing engine and nobody owns the handoff. Financial crime controls break the same way: the core banking system holds a customer the screening engine never sees, the payment message carries a field the monitoring rules never read, the risk assessment names a typology no scenario detects. The money moves through the gap, and every system involved reports itself green.

So the discipline is the same one. Reconcile the handoffs. Prove the controls do what the policy says they do. Write it down in a form somebody else can check. The difference is only who is asking — an auditor, or a regulator.

03 — The work, in detail

What we actually do
01

Assess & Assure

An enterprise-wide financial crime risk assessment has to cover five domains, and most cover three well: money laundering; terrorist financing and sanctions evasion; fraud and identity theft; bribery and corruption; market manipulation and insider trading. We score inherent risk across product, channel, customer type, geography and delivery, then test whether the control that is supposed to mitigate it actually operates — design effectiveness first, then operating effectiveness, on a real sample. That covers onboarding and KYC, CDD and the EDD triggers that are meant to escalate, ongoing review cycles, monitoring, screening, and the path from an alert to a filed report. Where a previous audit or examination left findings, we validate the corrective actions rather than take the closure memo at face value: an action that changed a procedure document but not the system behaviour has not closed anything.

You get a documented risk assessment methodology, a scored inherent-and-residual risk register, a control inventory mapped to the obligation it satisfies, testing workpapers with the sample and the results, a gap log with an owner and a date against each line, and a validation memo on the actions already claimed as closed. Without it, the risk assessment is a slide deck, and the first person to test the controls properly works for the regulator.

02

Regulatory Response & Remediation

Examinations are lost on evidence, not on intent. We run readiness ahead of one — assembling the evidence pack, reconciling what the policy claims against what the system does, and rehearsing the questions with the people who will have to answer them — and we run the remediation after one. That means a plan with milestones, owners and written exit criteria, so there is an agreed definition of done rather than a programme that runs until everyone is tired. Lookbacks are the heavy part: scoping the population and defending the scope, re-generating alerts against corrected logic, re-reviewing historic alerts to a consistent standard with a QA layer above it, and filing what the review turns up. And regulatory change is continuous — a new obligation is not implemented when the policy is updated, it is implemented when a control, a system field and a piece of evidence all exist for it.

You get a remediation plan with exit criteria, a lookback protocol that states its own methodology and sampling basis, a QA framework and the QA results, an evidence pack indexed to each finding, and a closure package that a third party can follow without asking you what you meant. Without it, remediation becomes a standing cost centre with no end date.

03

Transaction Monitoring & Screening

Scenario coverage is a two-way map. Every typology in the risk assessment should be detectable by at least one scenario; every scenario running in production should trace back to a typology someone still believes in. The gaps in both directions are the finding, and they are usually there. Then thresholds. We tune with above-the-line and below-the-line testing on a statistically defensible sample — below the line to prove that what you are choosing not to alert on is genuinely not interesting, above the line to show where productive alerts actually sit — and we write the tuning up so the change can be defended a year later by someone who was not in the room. Alert quality follows from it: the false-positive burden by scenario, productive-alert rates, alert-to-case and case-to-report conversion, customer segmentation that stops a corporate treasury being scored against retail behaviour. Model validation is a separate discipline and should be run by someone with no stake in the model passing — conceptual soundness, data integrity from source to detection layer, code against specification, outcomes analysis, and a performance-monitoring plan that does not depend on anyone remembering. Screening has its own failure modes: fuzzy-matching thresholds calibrated by test pack rather than by feel, name variation and transliteration, list ingestion and refresh, which message fields are screened and which quietly are not, PEP and adverse-media tiering, and false-negative testing — because a screening engine that has never been tested against a mutated true positive has never been tested at all.

You get a typology-to-scenario coverage matrix, scenario specifications, threshold tuning workpapers with the ATL/BTL samples and their outcomes, a model validation report with findings and severities, a screening calibration pack including the false-negative test set, and segmentation logic that is written down rather than inherited. Without it, you are paying analysts to close alerts generated by a threshold nobody chose.

04

Fraud Risk Management

Fraud is the domain where the control fights the business, and it loses unless the trade-off is made explicitly. We build the fraud risk assessment scheme by scheme — first-party and third-party, account takeover, authorised push payment and social engineering, synthetic and stolen identity, mule networks, chargeback abuse, and internal fraud, which is the one most registers are politely quiet about — and we set the control policy that follows: what is prevented at the front door, what is detected in flight, what is accepted and priced in. Red-flag analytics sit underneath: rules where the behaviour is known and stable, models where it is not, and an honest view of which is which. Every control gets a friction cost attached, because a fraud rule that also stops good customers is a revenue decision being made by the risk team without anyone saying so. And a response plan that only exists as a policy is a response plan that will be written for the first time during the incident.

You get a scheme-level fraud risk register, a fraud control policy with the prevent/detect/accept decision recorded against each scheme, a red-flag rule set with the tuning evidence behind it, a response and escalation playbook, and a loss and near-miss taxonomy so the next assessment has data instead of anecdotes. Without it, fraud strategy is a set of rules nobody can explain and nobody dares switch off.

05

Forensic Investigation

When something has already happened — internal fraud, a bribery or corruption allegation, misconduct, a data breach, a counterparty who turns out to be somebody else — the first hour matters more than the next month. We scope the question narrowly, preserve the evidence before it moves, collect and reconstruct, trace transactions to source and application of funds, follow the entity structure to the beneficial owner, and interview in an order that does not warn the wrong person. Third-party and counterparty due diligence works the same way in advance of the problem: ownership and control, sanctions and PEP exposure, adverse media with a stated search protocol rather than a search engine, and a red-flag escalation route that a relationship manager cannot quietly bypass. The output has to survive being read by people who were not there: a board, an auditor, a regulator, and sometimes a court.

You get an investigation plan and scope memo, a defensible chain of evidence, transaction tracing and entity mapping, a factual findings report that separates what is proven from what is inferred, a control-failure analysis so the same route cannot be used twice, and due-diligence files with a stated methodology. Without it, the investigation becomes the second incident.

06

Data, Analytics & Transformation

A monitoring engine can only see what reaches it. Before any scenario is worth tuning, the feed has to be right: which source systems deliver, which fields survive the journey, what the transformation layer silently drops, whether every account and every payment message that should be in scope is actually in scope. We map lineage end to end and reconcile record counts and values from the source system to the detection layer, because complete data is the only control that fails invisibly. On top of that sits model development and validation, and beneath it sits the operating model: where the three lines actually sit, who works an alert at level one and what they are permitted to close, handoffs to investigations, and which reviews are being done by a person purely because nobody has re-examined the process since it was designed. That is where cost of compliance is actually reduced — not by buying a better tool, but by removing the work that never needed a human.

You get a data lineage and field-level mapping specification, source-to-detection reconciliation controls, data quality rules with thresholds, model documentation and validation evidence, a target operating model with roles and decision rights, and a re-engineered alert-handling process with the manual steps that were removed listed one by one. Without it, you are tuning a model on data you have never proven arrives.

04 — The cost of compliance
Most of what a financial crime function costs is not spent on risk. It is spent on reviewing things that were never risk.

The budget grows every year and the estate gets no tighter, because the two are barely related. Headcount is added to work an alert queue that a threshold decision made, and the threshold decision is never revisited, because nobody can produce the evidence that would justify changing it. Tightening the controls and cutting the cost sound like opposites. In an untuned estate they are the same piece of work.

  • Alerts from scenarios that have never had a threshold tested, above or below the line.
  • Two or three scenarios firing on the same underlying behaviour, each generating its own case.
  • Screening set so wide that a common name is a hit every time, and so nobody trusts a hit.
  • Customer segmentation that scores a corporate treasury against retail thresholds.
  • Level-one analysts re-keying data the system already holds, into a case the system already opened.
  • A model nobody has validated, which means nobody is willing to be the person who changed it.
  • Reviews performed by a human because they always have been, not because a human adds judgement.

None of that is a licensing problem, so none of it is fixed by procurement. Each line is a piece of analysis, a documented decision, and evidence that the decision was sound. That is the work.

Regulated & nearly-regulated
01

Banks

Mature programmes with a large monitoring estate, an alert queue that grows faster than the balance sheet, and models that were validated once, some time ago, by someone who has left.

02

NBFCs

Obligations that have tightened faster than the control environment, and a compliance function that was staffed for a smaller book than the one it now supervises.

Payments companies

High volume, thin margin per transaction, and a monitoring layer that has to read message formats and merchant behaviour rather than a tidy customer relationship.

Fintechs

Growth that outran the control design, a sponsor bank or a licence application asking questions the current documentation cannot answer, and no appetite to hire an entire second-line function to find out.

05

Crypto businesses and VASPs

On-chain and off-chain evidence that has to be reconciled into one narrative, travel-rule obligations, and a supervisor who will want the methodology written down before the conclusions.

06

Insurers

Claims fraud and policy fraud sitting in one function, laundering and sanctions exposure sitting in another, and a risk assessment that treats them as unrelated.

If you already know which control is weak, we can go straight at it. If you only know that the alert volume has doubled and nobody can say why, that is a normal place to start.
