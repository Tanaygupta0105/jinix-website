02 — What each one is for

And what it is not
01 — Salesforce

CPQ & Revenue Cloud

Its real advantage is that it lives where the sales team already lives. The quote, the approval chain, the opportunity, the amendment and the renewal all sit on records the business already knows how to read, which means the commercial process can be governed rather than merely observed. If the constraint you are trying to fix is that deals are being quoted in shapes the company never agreed to sell, this is where you fix it.

We implement the product catalog, bundles and options, price books, price rules and discount schedules, the approval matrix, quote and order document templates, amendment and renewal logic, and the handoff into billing.

Do not buy it if your problem is downstream. It is a quoting engine, not a billing engine; asking it to rate high-volume usage or to carry ASC 606 at scale produces a large surface of custom code and a fragile close. It also assumes an owner. The configuration surface is deep, and teams without a named administrator find that it drifts.

02 — Zuora

Billing & RevPro

Zuora treats the subscription as a first-class object, which sounds obvious until you have watched a general-purpose billing system try to amend one. Rate plans, mid-term changes, proration, co-terming, ramps and renewals behave the way a subscription business actually behaves, and RevPro sits behind it so that ASC 606 is a system output rather than a quarterly act of endurance. It is the strongest fit when the business is genuinely subscription-shaped and finance needs recognition to be repeatable.

We implement the product catalog and rate plans, amendment and order logic, invoicing, payment runs, dunning, Workflow automation, and in RevPro the performance obligations, SSP and allocation, recognition schedules and the deferred revenue waterfall.

Do not buy it if your pricing is essentially flat and your volume is modest. The model carries an operating cost, and a business with three price points will pay it without collecting the benefit. It also has opinions. Where your rating logic is genuinely unusual, you will spend the project arguing with the data model, and the data model wins.

03 — BillingPlatform

Rating, Usage & Invoicing

This is the one we reach for when the pricing is strange. High-volume metered usage, mediation before rating, tapered and hybrid models, per-customer commercial terms that refuse to generalise, invoices that have to be assembled rather than printed. Its configuration model is more open than most, and that openness is the point: it can express rating logic that other engines can only approximate with custom code sitting outside the system of record.

We implement usage ingestion, deduplication and aggregation, the rating and pricing engine, commitments and overage, invoice composition and presentment, and the receivables and collections path behind it.

Do not buy it if you want an opinionated subscription flow out of the box and a short implementation. Flexibility is not free. It transfers the design decisions to you, and it needs someone on your side who will make those decisions and write them down. Bought as a shortcut, it becomes an empty room.

04 — Conga

CLM, CPQ & Document Gen

Conga earns its place when the negotiated paper is where the risk lives. Non-standard terms, redlines, obligations, auto-renewal and termination clauses, uplift caps that somebody agreed to three years ago and nobody has read since. Put the contract at the centre and hang the quote off it, and the commitments in the document and the commitments in the billing system finally describe the same deal. Document generation is also the most reliable route we know to paperwork that matches the configuration behind it.

We implement the clause library and templates, negotiation and approval workflow, obligation tracking, CPQ where it belongs alongside CLM, and document generation across quote, order form and contract.

Do not buy it if your contracts are standard order-form terms signed without negotiation. CLM overhead does not repay itself on paper nobody argues about. And choosing Conga CPQ over Salesforce CPQ is a real decision with real consequences for where your revenue data lives. If there is no contract-lifecycle reason for it, it is usually the wrong reason.

05 — NetSuite

ARM & SuiteBilling

The argument for NetSuite is proximity to the ledger. If it is already your ERP, ARM gives you revenue arrangements, fair-value allocation and recognition schedules in the same system as the GL, which removes an entire interface and the reconciliation that goes with it. Closes get shorter for an unglamorous reason: there is one fewer place for the numbers to disagree. SuiteBilling handles straightforward subscription and usage billing without adding another vendor to the estate.

We implement ARM revenue arrangements, fair value and allocation, revenue plans and reclassification, SuiteBilling subscriptions, rating and invoicing, and the close and GL alignment around both.

Do not buy it if your rating requirements are heavy. Very high usage volumes, elaborate pricing or complex invoice composition will find the edges of SuiteBilling, and pushing it past its shape shows up as manual work at close. It is a competent biller attached to an excellent ledger, not a specialist rating engine.

06 — None of them

The answer nobody sells

Not every revenue problem is a platform problem. A large share of what an assessment turns up is process and data: a product catalog nobody owns, an approval matrix that exists in a policy document and nowhere in the system, a usage feed that arrives twice on the first of the month. New software does not fix any of that. It capitalises it, and then the failure has a licence fee attached.

If the honest answer is that your current stack, properly configured and properly owned, will hold for another two years, we will say so, and the assessment will say so in writing. That is a shorter engagement and a smaller invoice. It is also the reason the recommendation is worth reading.

03 — How we choose

Seven questions

The selection is
usually decided
before the demos.

Vendor evaluations tend to compare feature lists, which is the one thing every platform can win. These are the questions that actually determine the answer, and most of them are about you rather than the software.

  1. Deal complexity. How far does a real deal sit from list price, and how many people have to agree before it can be sent?
  2. Pricing model. Recurring, consumption, or a hybrid that changes mid-term — and where does the usage data come from, in what volume, and how much do you trust it?
  3. Scale. Invoice and line-item volume, legal entities, currencies, tax jurisdictions. Scale rules platforms out far more often than it rules them in.
  4. The stack you already have. The CRM and the ERP already in place are the two heaviest facts in the room. Ignoring them buys you an integration project you did not budget for.
  5. Who owns it on the Monday after go-live. A platform that needs a specialist and a team that has no specialist is a decision that fails eighteen months later, quietly.
  6. Time to value. What has to be live before the next audit, the next close, or the next diligence process? A perfect target state that arrives a quarter late is a worse plan than a partial one that arrives on time.
  7. Exit cost. Every platform is a bet. Ask early how you would get your subscriptions, invoices and revenue schedules back out of it, because one day somebody will have to.
