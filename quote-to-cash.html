02 — The work, in detail

What we actually do
01

CPQ Implementation

A CPQ project is a modelling project before it is a software project. We build the product catalog — products, options, bundles, price books, discount schedules — so a quote can only be assembled in shapes the business has agreed to sell. Then the guardrails: an approval matrix that encodes real discount authority by role and by margin floor, price rules that hold under pressure, and quote templates that produce a document matching the configuration behind it. The hard parts are always the same — ramp deals, multi-year uplifts, mid-term amendments, co-terming a new order to an existing subscription — and they are where most implementations quietly give up and hand the problem to a spreadsheet.

You get a documented catalog data model, the full price-rule and approval inventory, quote and order document templates, UAT scripts, and an administrator trained to own the configuration after we leave. Without it, every deal is bespoke, discount authority lives in a policy document and nowhere in the system, and billing inherits configurations it cannot rate.

02

Subscription & Usage Billing

Billing is where the promises made in the quote get tested. We configure the rating engine — recurring charges, tiered, volume and graduated pricing, committed use with overage, minimum commitments — and the usage path that feeds it: ingestion, deduplication, aggregation, and the rating run itself. Proration is the part that bites. A mid-term upgrade, a downgrade, a quantity change and a co-term all have to land on the invoice the way a controller would expect them to, in the right period and the right currency. Then invoicing, tax hooks, payment runs, dunning, retry logic, credits and rebills, across entities that rarely share a chart of accounts.

You get the rating configuration and the test cases that prove it, invoice templates, a dunning and collections policy that exists in the system rather than in someone's habits, and an order-to-invoice reconciliation you can run every month. Without it, usage arrives twice or not at all, proration is defensible to nobody, and the disputes queue becomes a second finance team.

03

Revenue Recognition

ASC 606 is five steps, and every one of them is a system decision. We implement contract identification and combination, decomposition into performance obligations, transaction price including variable consideration and its constraint, standalone selling price analysis with the resulting allocation and carve-outs, and recognition against each obligation as it is satisfied. The work nobody budgets for is contract modification: deciding whether a change is a separate contract, a prospective reallocation or a cumulative catch-up, and making the system give the same answer twice. Around it sits the deferred revenue waterfall, the split between contract asset and contract liability, and the roll-forward the auditor will ask for on the first morning.

You get an SSP methodology memo, performance-obligation logic held in configuration rather than in one person's head, recognition schedules, waterfall and roll-forward reporting, and an audit trail that runs from a revenue line back to the order that created it. Without it, the close is a spreadsheet, and the spreadsheet is a person.

04

Q2C Architecture

Before anything is built, someone has to decide where the truth lives. Which system owns the account, the product, the price, the order, the subscription, the invoice, the revenue schedule — and what is supposed to happen when two of them disagree. We map the current state, design the target, and write the integration contracts: what crosses each boundary, in what shape, what makes a message idempotent, and how a failed one is reprocessed rather than lost. The order is usually the right boundary between the commercial systems and the financial ones. Most stacks we are asked to look at do not treat it as a boundary at all, which is why the CRM and the ledger have quietly stopped agreeing.

You get a current-state map, a target architecture, an integration and data-flow specification, a reconciliation control matrix across CRM, CPQ, billing and the ledger, and a sequenced roadmap with its dependencies made explicit. Without it, you buy good platforms and wire them together with assumptions.

05

Platform Migration

Migrations fail on the things that were already in flight. Subscriptions part-way through a term, amendments part-applied, open receivables, historical invoices somebody will need to reprint, and deferred revenue balances that must land in the new system exactly as the old one left them. We build the field-level mapping specification, migrate in waves, and dual-run: the legacy system and the new one bill the same period in parallel, and we reconcile at invoice-line level until every difference is explained rather than tolerated. Cutover happens after that, not before, and with a rollback path written down while it is still hypothetical.

You get a migration playbook, the mapping specification, dual-run reconciliation reports, a cutover runbook, and a decommissioning plan so the legacy stack does not linger for two years as a read-only ghost. Without it, the first close on the new platform is the test, and your customers are the ones who find the bugs.

06

Fractional RevOps

The build ends and the system keeps changing. A new product, a new pricing model, a new entity, a new tax jurisdiction, an auditor with a question nobody has been asked before. Most companies discover after go-live that nobody owns the revenue stack: the implementation team has gone, the administrator already has three other systems, and the backlog is a message thread. We take that ownership on a standing monthly basis — release management, configuration changes, month-end close support, escalation cover, and a quarterly view of what should change next and what should be left alone.

You get one named person who knows your configuration, a maintained backlog, and documentation that does not depend on us being there. Without it, configuration drifts, workarounds harden into process, and in three years the platform is blamed for decisions nobody recorded.
