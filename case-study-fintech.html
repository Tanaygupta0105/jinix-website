Case study — Global fintech

Five billing systems,
folded into one,
with nobody billed wrong.

A global fintech had accumulated five billing systems, one per era of the company's growth. We consolidated them into a single engine and moved more than 50,000 users across without a billing error. Month-end close went from eight days to four, and the finance team stopped spending eighty hours a month proving that the systems disagreed with each other.

01 — The challenge

Five systems, one customer

Nobody sets out to run five billing systems. You inherit one, build one for a product line the others could not price, and keep the original alive because a cohort of customers is still on it. Each decision was reasonable when it was made. Together they meant the answer to what do we bill this customer depended on which system you asked.

  • Five billing engines, each with its own product catalog, its own pricing logic, and its own idea of what a customer record was.
  • Manual reconciliation between them consumed more than 80 hours a month — roughly half a full-time person, spent establishing which system was right.
  • Month-end close ran to eight days, because the close could not start until the reconciliation had finished.
  • Reporting had no single source of truth. Revenue by product, market or cohort was assembled by hand, and two people assembling it could return two different answers.
  • Launching in a new market meant configuring it five times, or picking one system and accepting the fragmentation that followed.
  • Any consolidation had to happen without a billing error. These were live financial products, and in that sector an incorrect invoice is not a cosmetic failure.

02 — What we built

04 workstreams
01

One catalog, five dialects

Consolidation is a modelling problem before it is a migration problem. We reconciled five product catalogs into one — products, plans, charge models, price books, discount structures — and recorded, for every legacy construct, either the target construct it mapped to or the reason it should not survive. A good number did not survive: duplicated plans, pricing no live customer was on, and charges that existed only because one system could not express what another could. That inventory became the specification for everything downstream.

02

Migration in waves, with a dual-run

We did not cut 50,000 users over at once. We migrated in waves, simplest cohort first, and dual-ran each one: the legacy system and the new engine billed the same period in parallel, and we reconciled at invoice-line level until every difference was explained rather than tolerated. A wave cut over only when its dual-run was clean. The in-flight cases were the entire difficulty — subscriptions part-way through a term, part-applied amendments, open receivables, deferred balances that had to land in the new system exactly as the old one left them — and they were resolved before cutover, not discovered after it.

03

The order as the boundary

Five systems were the symptom. The untreated seam between the commercial stack and the financial one was the cause. We made the order the boundary: an approved order now arrives at the billing engine in one shape, is rated once, invoices once, and posts to the ledger with a trail back to the order line that produced it. The 80 hours a month of reconciliation were not made faster. They were made unnecessary, because the systems being compared became one system.

04

Reporting on one truth

We rebuilt revenue reporting directly on the consolidated engine — by product, market, cohort and entity, drawn from the same records the invoices are generated from, with the roll-forward an auditor asks for on the first morning. Two people asking the same question now get the same answer. That sounds like a low bar until you have spent a quarter without it.

The result the client valued most is not in the grid, because it is not a percentage. With one catalog and one billing engine, entering a new market became a configuration rather than a project. They expanded into twelve new markets in the following six months — something the five-system estate had made expensive enough to keep deferring.

Two honest notes on the figures. Billing accuracy is 98%, not 100%: the residual is corrections driven by upstream data and by edge cases in the older cohorts, and we would rather print the real number than a rounder one. And the $2.4M is a run-rate saving, annualised from retired licences and recovered finance hours. It is not cash that appeared in a single quarter.
